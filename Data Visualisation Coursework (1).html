Young Men's Mental Wellbeing in the UK¶

Introduction¶

This report analyses the state of young people's mental wellbeing in the UK. Using a varity of different aspects to gain a more holsitc picture of the mental wellbeing of young men and women. Aspects such as relational connections and community, satisifcation in leisure time and overall mental wellbeing. A key focus is to understand the state mental wellbeing of men in the UK and how this compares to women's, both recently and across the years.

My intrest in this area of men's mental wellbeing was sparked by one of my favourite UK music artists, Guvna B. In his most recent book, Unspoken: Toxic Masculinity and How I Faced the Man Within the Man, he offered a vulnerable perspective on his own journey with expressing his feelings and how as a man he found it harder to show weakness in his ubpringing and how this impacted his mental wellbeing. In his newest album, The Village is on Fire, he explores the need for community and people to reach out to when in need. These bodies of work resonnated with me and raised my interest in the topic of mental well-being for men in particualr, for myself and others. I began to be curious about how well, or not well, I prioritise my own well-being and decided to research how other people do as well.

Initially, we will provide context for our concept domain. Then data collection is described along justifications for my chosen dataset and an evaluation of its limitations. Afterwards, I will go into the analysis and results relating to my research questions, ending with an evaluation of the overall visualisation process.

Research topic and context¶

Topic definitions:¶

Mental wellbeing: "Mental wellbeing derives from psychological functioning, which includes the ability to develop and maintain mutually beneficial relationships, and from levels of happiness and contentment with life, usually measured as life satisfaction. Psychological functioning includes the ability to maintain a sense of autonomy, agency, self-acceptance, self-esteem, personal growth and purpose in life. Mental wellbeing is more than the outcome of treating or preventing mental illness." (Warwick-Edinburgh Mental Wellbeing Scale, 2006)

Eudaimonic perspective: An "Eudaimonic perspective of wellbeing focuses on psychological functioning, good relationships with others and self-realisation. This is the development of human potential that, when realised, results in positive functioning in life, and covers a wide range of cognitive aspects of mental health." (Warwick-Edinburgh Mental Wellbeing Scale, 2006)

Warwick edinburgh mental wellbeing scale (WEMWBS):

  • Overview : "The Warwick-Edinburgh Mental Wellbeing Scales were developed to enable the measuring of mental wellbeing in the general population and the evaluation of projects, programmes and policies which aim to improve mental wellbeing The items are all worded positively and cover both feeling and functioning aspects of mental wellbeing, thereby making the concept more accessible. The scale has been widely used nationally and internationally for monitoring, evaluating projects and programmes and investigating the determinants of mental wellbeing."
  • How it is used : "WEMWBS is a 14-item scale covering subjective wellbeing and psychological functioning, in which all items are worded positively and address aspects of positive mental health. The scale is scored by summing the response to each item answered on a 1 to 5 Likert scale. The minimum scale score is 14 and the maximum is 70. WEMWBS was initially validated for use in the UK with those aged 16 and above, involving surveys in both student and general population samples, and focus groups. It has now been widely validated in different populations and languages other than English."
  • Origins : The WarwickEdinburgh Mental Well-being Scale was funded by the Scottish Government National Programme for Improving Mental Health and Wellbeing, commissioned by NHS Health Scotland, developed by the University of Warwick and the University of Edinburgh, and is jointly owned by NHS Health Scotland, the University of Warwick and the University of Edinburgh. (Professor Stewart-Brown and Dr Janmohamed, 2015)

Short Warwick edinburgh mental wellbeing scale (SWEMWBS): SWEMWBS is a short version of the Warwick-Edinburgh Mental Wellbeing Scale, containing a 7-item scale instead of the full 14.

Leisure time satisfaction: positive perceptions or feelings that an individual forms, elicits, or gains as a result of engaging in leisure activities and choices. It is the degree to which one is presently content or pleased with her general leisure experiences and situations. This positive feeling of pleasure results from the satisfaction of felt or unfelt needs of the individual. (Beard & Ragheb, 1980)

Be there (for someone): Idiom, to be available to provide help and support for someone. (Cambridge Dictionary)

Data collection¶

The dataset was obtained from the Office of National Statistics (ONS) chosen because it focuses on mental well-being over mental health which is outside of my desired scope. I want to focus on exploring young peoples ability to function well and respond to external challenges as apposed to the presence or absence of mental illnesses.

This dataset also uses a trusted scale for mental wellbeing, the WEMWBS, which is specically designed to measure changes in mental well being. This is again opposed to other scales used in questionnaires that, while important, are designed to identify mental health disorders such as scizopherina, depression and suicide. The noticeable difference with the WEMBS scale is the focus on positivity, holistic growth and development, which has potential to be relevant wider range of young people; those dealing with mental health issues and those who are not. This scale being used made the dataset stick out to me.

Lastly, this specific dataset is not well reported on, there is space for me to explore this dataset.

When evaluating the data I noticed the suitablity of the questions asked, to the requirements of the report and my aforementioned definiton of mental-wellbeing. Requirements such as:

  • the established WEMWBS to measure mental-wellbing,
  • physical activity and leisure included as well as,
  • measures on connectivity and relationships.
  • The dataset also compared men and women.
  • Additionally, it includes enough time series data to show changes

One limitiations of the dataset is that the time series data stopping before the COVID-19 pandemic, the dataset's most recent entry was March 2020. It would have been intresting to see how the pandemic impacted the mental-wellbeing, however, this might need to be its own dedicated study. Another limitation would be the data being edited in terms of averages and percentages. Not allowing us to have access to the raw data from these questioniares. This makes it harder to get detailed descriptive statistics.

Data overview and pre-processing¶

The dataset contains a wide range of responses from young people across the UK to questions regarding their mental well-being. Due to this, the data composition is mostly ordinal categorical variables, likert scale responses, for example: Completely dissatisfied to Completely satisfied.

We do have other variable data types, such as the time series data. This is a quatitative interval data type used in almost every sheet, showing changes in responses over-time,. The variability of the time series is set to annual observations as a time span.

We also have ratio in the percentages and the SWEMWBS variable; The percentages show the proportion of responses to certain questions. And although SWEMWBS is a questionaire, the responses on the point-scale are totaled and calculated as a metric score, we have the average for each year. Laslty, we have genders as the only nominal variable.

## User-defined function for inital data cleaning, refer back to for keyword arguments
# we use slicing with the given arguments to removed various repeated unnecessary values. 

def remove_na_values(dataset, first_rows, last_column, middle_columns, negative_bottom ):
    
    # deleting the first set of rows of grpahics added by the editor to show overall change
    dataset.drop(index = dataset.index[0:first_rows], inplace = True)
    
    # removing all unnecessary columns from last_column onwards
    dataset.drop(columns=dataset.columns[last_column:], inplace = True)
    
    # check if there are middle columns to delete, if so remove them
    if middle_columns != None:
    # deleting formatting rows of blank space inbetween values
        dataset.drop(columns=middle_columns, inplace = True)
        
    
    # deleting the rows with editors notes at the bottom of the dataset
    dataset.drop(index=dataset.index[negative_bottom:], inplace=True)
    
    # Resetting the index back to zero to be more useful
    dataset.reset_index(drop=True,inplace=True)
    
    # Making the header to the likert scale options instead of 'Unnamed'
    dataset.columns = dataset.iloc[0]
    
    # Correcting a subscript error
    dataset.replace('2009-103', '2009-10', inplace = True)
    
    return dataset

Our main data questions for this visualisation was

  • how many young men feel like they have someone to rely on?
  • how does this compare to women in the same age range?

When analysing the data I noticed a difference in how many men agreed to having someone to rely on and ask for help when compared to women. In every postive response, women reported considerably higher levels of having someone to rely on. I wanted to clearly visualise this difference as an answer to the question above.

I choose to use a stacked bar chart, as the chart type that is often used when comparing nomial cateogories (in our example, male and female). The stacked bar chart is also suitable to the nature of our data, containing likert scale responses, categorical ordinal variables. The reader can see the logical sequence of responses. In addition, stacked bars helps show the responses as a percentage in a clear and concise way. The tone in this visualisation was more emotive, with a focus on the visual difference in responses over high accuracy, which is another reason for this bar chart design choice. (kirk 2012)

I utlised a slope graph, called a pointplot in Seaborn. The aim of this visualisation was to focus on the gap between responses in even greater detail. Showing the change across one time-frame as conistently lower for men than women when it comes to connectivness. Noted, this is one form of measuring connectiveness, but it does justify further research into why this gap is present and how we can maybe reduce it.

Conclusions¶

Key findings¶

  • Over time men and womens overall health is similar
  • Men seem more satisified with leisure than women
  • There is a slight gap in terms of connectivity of men and women, favouring women. This might spark more research and study into solutions to reduce this gap and help young men be more connected.

The focus of the dataset was to show changes in attiudes of young people over time. This restricted me in terms of making my own observations. If I was to do this assingment again, I would have found the dataset closest to the raw data. I did end up finding this dataset, but far to late in the report process to ultise.

Secondly, I learnt the importance of data cleaning, a lot of my plans for visualisations were limited by poor labeling, indexing and incorrect typing of data. Especially as my visualisation tool, pandas, assumes tidy data.

// Run this cell to update your word count.

function wordcount() {
    let wordCount = 0
    let extraCount = 0
    let mainBody = true

    let cells = Jupyter.notebook.get_cells()
    cells.forEach((cell) => {
        if (cell.cell_type == 'markdown') {
            let text = cell.get_text()
            // Stop counting as main body when get to References or Appendices.
            if (text.startsWith('## References') ||
                text.startsWith('## Appendices')) {
                mainBody = false
            }
            if (text.startsWith('## Word Count')) {
                text = ''
            }
            if (text) {
                let words = text.toLowerCase().match(/\b[a-z\d]+\b/g)
                if (words) {
                    let cellCount = words.length
                    if (mainBody) {
                        wordCount += cellCount
                    } else {
                        extraCount += cellCount
                    }
                }
            }
        }
    })
    return [wordCount, extraCount]
}
